# Yggdrasil & Ratatoskr — Lore Reference

Source notes for the lore that anchors the Ratatoskr brand. Useful
when:
- Writing copy that references Norse mythology and you want to get
  details right without hand-waving
- Briefing a designer who is unfamiliar with the lore
- Defending the metaphor in a marketing context ("why a squirrel?")

This file deliberately **does not** include any copyrighted artwork.
It links out to public-domain references that future contributors
can use freely. See the bottom of this file for a list.

---

## Ratatoskr in three sentences

Ratatoskr is a squirrel in Norse mythology who lives on Yggdrasil,
the world tree. He runs up and down the trunk carrying messages —
typically insults — between the unnamed eagle perched at the crown
and Niðhöggr, the serpent gnawing at the roots. He appears in both
the *Poetic Edda* (Grímnismál, stanzas 32 and 35) and the *Prose
Edda* (Gylfaginning, chapter 16).

That's it. That's the entire mythological footprint. The brevity is
useful — it leaves room for the project to define what Ratatoskr
*does* in a modern context without contradicting the canon.

## Yggdrasil — the world tree

Yggdrasil is an immense ash tree (some sources say evergreen) at the
center of the Norse cosmos. It connects:

- **Asgard** — realm of the Æsir gods, at or near the crown
- **Vanaheim** — realm of the Vanir gods
- **Alfheim** — realm of the light elves
- **Midgard** — realm of humans, in the middle
- **Jotunheim** — realm of giants
- **Svartalfheim / Nidavellir** — realm of dwarves and dark elves
- **Muspelheim** — realm of fire and the fire giants
- **Niflheim** — realm of mist and ice
- **Helheim** — realm of the dead

Three roots reach down. Beneath them are wells of wisdom (Mímir's
well), fate (the well of the Norns), and the underworld. Niðhöggr,
the serpent, lives at the root that reaches into Niflheim and
chews on it.

The eagle at the top is unnamed in surviving sources. A hawk named
Veðrfölnir sits between its eyes. (You can ignore the hawk for
brand purposes — Ratatoskr's traffic is between the eagle and the
serpent.)

## Why this maps onto the project

The map is in `brand-direction.md` already. In short:

- The tree is **the multi-chain ecosystem**. Branches reach into
  different worlds — different chains, different DeFi venues.
- Ratatoskr is **the bridge / messenger layer**. He runs the trunk.
  He doesn't live in either Asgard or Niflheim; he transits between
  them.
- The eagle and serpent are **the chains being bridged** — currently
  Alephium (eagle, sky-coded, high-throughput, modern) and the RATR
  L1 itself (serpent at the roots, the foundational PoW chain that
  the ecosystem grows out of).
- The project's larger umbrella, **EnchantedForestDeFi**, is the
  forest where Yggdrasil grows.

The metaphor is durable: as the project adds bridges to other
chains, each new chain becomes another world hanging from
Yggdrasil's branches. Ratatoskr's job — message-bearing — doesn't
change; the destinations do.

## Things the lore lets us claim

These are all defensible from the source material and useful as
narrative beats:

- **"Carries messages between chains."** Direct from canon.
  Ratatoskr is literally a messenger.
- **"Connects sky and root."** The eagle is at the crown, Niðhöggr
  is at the roots. Ratatoskr is the only entity that travels both.
- **"Mischievous, fast, persistent."** The lore notes that Ratatoskr
  stirs up enmity by carrying insults — interpret as "moves quickly,
  enables interaction between parties that otherwise would not
  speak." (Modern-day: bridge enables interaction between chains
  that otherwise would not communicate.)
- **"Part of an old, deeply rooted system."** Yggdrasil is described
  as ancient and central. The brand can lean on "rooted in
  established fundamentals" (PoW, masternodes, MIT-licensed
  Bitcoin lineage) without seeming cosplay-y.

## Things the lore does NOT support

Avoid these — they contradict canon and a Norse-literate reader will
roll their eyes:

- ❌ Ratatoskr as a warrior / fighter. He is a messenger, not a
  combatant.
- ❌ Ratatoskr carrying gold or treasure. The lore is about words,
  not wealth. (For brand purposes, "messages" can stretch to
  "transactions / tokens" — those *are* messages on a chain — but
  don't show him hoarding coins.)
- ❌ Ratatoskr at rest / sleeping / posed heroically. He is in
  motion. Always.
- ❌ Confusion between Ratatoskr and other Norse animals.
  Specifically: Sleipnir (Odin's eight-legged horse), Hugin and
  Munin (Odin's ravens), Geri and Freki (Odin's wolves) — none of
  these are Ratatoskr.

## Public-domain references (for designers)

When you need visual reference material that is safe to commit, link
to, or trace, these are public-domain options. All are out of
copyright (creators died >70 years ago) and freely usable:

- **Oluf Olufsen Bagge, "Yggdrasil" (1847)** — colored engraving
  showing the tree, the worlds hanging from it, Ratatoskr on the
  trunk, Niðhöggr at the roots. The most "complete" classical
  depiction. Available on Wikimedia Commons.
  Search: `Oluf Olufsen Bagge Yggdrasil 1847`

- **Lorenz Frølich illustrations (1895)** — Frølich illustrated a
  Danish edition of the *Prose Edda*. His Ratatoskr drawings are
  loose, expressive, and clearly show the squirrel in motion on
  Yggdrasil. Available on Wikimedia Commons under his name.

- **Friedrich Wilhelm Heine, "Yggdrasil" (1882)** — wood engraving,
  monochrome, dramatic. Useful for hero-art compositions rather
  than icon work.

- **Manuscript marginalia, Codex Trajectinus (17th century)** — a
  paper copy of the *Prose Edda* with line-drawing illustrations
  that include Yggdrasil with named creatures. Public domain due to
  age. Search: `Codex Trajectinus Yggdrasil`.

- **Wikipedia "Ratatoskr" article** — text is CC-BY-SA. The article
  itself includes a small public-domain illustration from the
  17th-century Icelandic manuscript AM 738 4to. That manuscript's
  Ratatoskr drawing is rough and folkloric — useful as silhouette
  reference at small sizes.

A reasonable practice when committing reference imagery:

```
doc/brand/refs/
  bagge-1847-yggdrasil.png          # public domain
  frolich-1895-ratatoskr.png        # public domain
  am738-4to-ratatoskr.png           # public domain
  REFERENCE-ATTRIBUTION.md          # source URL + license per file
```

The refs folder is **for designers' eyes only** — these are not
project assets and shouldn't be referenced from the website or the
wallet. Their purpose is to anchor the new commissioned/generated
art in the lore.

## Stock-photo & licensed-image policy

If the operator finds a modern Yggdrasil illustration they love
(stock photo, artist's portfolio, etc.), the workflow is:

1. **License it.** Pay the appropriate fee, save the receipt /
   license document somewhere durable (1Password / GPM / a folder
   in B2 backups).
2. **Note the license terms** in `doc/assets-attribution.md` —
   what's allowed (web use? merch? print?) and any required
   credit line.
3. **Commit the image** to `doc/brand/refs/licensed/` with the
   license document as a sibling.
4. **Don't commit** the watermarked preview version — only the
   licensed full-resolution version.

Alternatively, **commission a derivative work** based on the
reference. That gives you full IP ownership, which is cleaner for
long-term brand use than a single-use stock license.

## Pronunciation note

For voice-over / video content: **RAT-uh-toskr** is the most common
modern English pronunciation. Some sources prefer **rah-tah-TOSKR**
(closer to Old Norse). Either is fine — the project does not need to
prescribe one.

The token symbol is **RATR**, pronounced as letters: "R-A-T-R" or
"rater." Avoid pronouncing RATR as the full word "Ratatoskr" — they
are visually linked but the symbol is meant to be quick to type and
say.

## Further reading

- *The Poetic Edda*, Grímnismál stanzas 32 and 35
- *The Prose Edda* (Snorri Sturluson, 13th c.), Gylfaginning ch. 16
- John Lindow, *Norse Mythology: A Guide to the Gods, Heroes,
  Rituals, and Beliefs* (Oxford University Press) — readable
  modern reference, single chapter on Yggdrasil
- Rudolf Simek, *Dictionary of Northern Mythology* — entry on
  Ratatoskr is short but cites all primary sources

Both Lindow and Simek are still in copyright; consult at a library
or buy directly. The Eddas themselves are public domain in modern
translation; the Henry Adams Bellows (*Poetic*) and Anthony Faulkes
(*Prose*) translations are widely available online.
